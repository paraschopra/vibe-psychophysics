Background

Michael Posner (1980) developed the cueing paradigm to study covert attention — the ability to shift the focus of attention without moving the eyes. A cue directs attention to a location, followed by a target that appears either at the cued location (valid) or elsewhere (invalid).

Key Findings

  • Valid cues speed responses by 20–50 ms compared to invalid cues
  • Endogenous (voluntary) attention is directed by central arrow cues
  • Inhibition of return (IOR) can occur at long SOAs: previously attended locations become inhibited

This Experiment

You will see three boxes. A central arrow cue will point left or right. After a brief delay (300 ms SOA), a target (*) will appear in one of the side boxes. The cue is valid 70% of the time, creating a strong expectation.

References

  1. Posner, M.I. (1980). Orienting of attention. Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology, 32, 3–25.
  2. Posner, M.I. & Cohen, Y. (1984). Components of visual orienting. In H. Bouma & D. Bouwhuis (Eds.), Attention and Performance X, pp. 531–556.
  3. Klein, R.M. (2000). Inhibition of return. Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 4, 138–147.

Posner Cueing Task

Three boxes will appear on screen. A central arrow (← or →) will point to one side.

After a brief delay, a target * will appear in one of the side boxes.

Press Space as fast as possible when you see the target.

The arrow is usually (70%) correct, so it helps to pay attention to where it points — but don't move your eyes, just shift your attention.

There are 80 trials total. Focus on speed while maintaining attention to the cue.
