Background

The Müller-Lyer illusion (1889) is perhaps the most famous geometric illusion. Two lines of equal physical length appear different depending on the direction of the arrowhead-like “fins” at their endpoints:

  • Outward fins (>—<): the line appears longer than it really is
  • Inward fins (<—>): the line appears shorter

Explanations

Several theories have been proposed: misapplied constancy scaling (Gregory, 1963) suggests outward fins resemble the inside corner of a room (perceived as farther away, thus scaled up), while inward fins resemble an outside corner. Confusion theory suggests the visual system averages the overall extent of the figure.
