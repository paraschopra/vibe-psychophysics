Background

Weber's Law (Ernst Heinrich Weber, 1834) states that the just-noticeable difference (JND) between two stimuli is proportional to the magnitude of the stimuli:

ΔI / I = k

where ΔI is the JND, I is the standard intensity, and the constant k is the Weber fraction.

This means larger stimuli need larger differences to be discriminable. A 100 px line needs a larger pixel difference to tell apart than a 50 px line.

Method of Constant Stimuli

Comparison stimuli at fixed increments around the standard are presented repeatedly. The JND is estimated from the psychometric function — the S-shaped curve relating stimulus difference to proportion of “comparison longer” responses.

References

  1. Weber, E.H. (1834). De pulsu, resorptione, auditu et tactu. Leipzig: Koehler.
  2. Gescheider, G.A. (1997). Psychophysics: The Fundamentals (3rd ed.). Lawrence Erlbaum.
