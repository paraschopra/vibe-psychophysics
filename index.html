Psychophysics Lab

A collection of classic perception and cognition experiments you can run in your browser. Each experiment includes background theory, an interactive paradigm, data collection, and results visualization.

10 experiments · No installation required · Data stored locally

Visual Visual Perception

Müller-Lyer Illusion

Do arrow fins make lines look longer or shorter? Adjust a comparison line to match a standard and measure your susceptibility to this classic size illusion.

18 trials ~3 min Müller-Lyer, 1889
Ebbinghaus Illusion

Surrounding circles distort perceived size. Adjust a central circle to match a standard and see how context shapes perception.

18 trials ~3 min Ebbinghaus, 1902
Change Blindness

Can you spot the change? Scenes flicker between two versions with a brief blank. Find the changing object — it's harder than you think.

8 trials ~5 min Rensink et al., 1997

Attention Attention & Cognition

Stroop Effect

Name the ink color of color words. When word and color conflict, your brain's automatic reading interferes — the most replicated effect in psychology.

48 trials ~3 min Stroop, 1935
Posner Cueing Task

An arrow cue directs your attention. Detect targets faster at cued locations and measure the cost of invalid cues on covert attention.

80 trials ~4 min Posner, 1980
Visual Search / Pop-Out

Find a red vertical bar among distractors. Feature search is instant (pop-out!), but conjunction search requires slow, serial inspection.

80 trials ~6 min Treisman & Gelade, 1980
Attentional Blink

Spot two targets in a rapid letter stream. The second target is often “blinked” — missed for 200–500 ms after the first, revealing a bottleneck in temporal attention.

56 trials ~8 min Raymond et al., 1992

Auditory Auditory Perception

Shepard Tone Illusion

Listen to pairs of Shepard tones — layered octaves that create the illusion of endlessly ascending pitch. The tritone paradox reveals your personal pitch template.

24 trials ~5 min Shepard, 1964

Methods Psychophysical Methods

Stevens' Power Law

Rate the perceived length of lines relative to a standard. Your data reveals whether perception follows Stevens' power law: ψ = k · In.

30 trials ~4 min Stevens, 1957

RT Reaction Time

Simple & Choice RT

The most fundamental measure in experimental psychology. Compare simple (one response) vs. choice (two responses) reaction time and see Hick's Law in action.

60 trials ~3 min Hick, 1952; Donders, 1868

Data Management

All experiment data is stored in your browser's localStorage. You can export data as CSV or JSON from each experiment's results page.
